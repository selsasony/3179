CAUSES OF DEATHS IN AUSTRALIA

Australia’s leading causes of death reveal the diverse range of internal and external forces that shape mortality. Accidents, self-harm, and other non-natural causes account for a significant portion of deaths, reflecting both preventable tragedies and systemic societal challenges. Each death represents not just a statistic, but a life impacted by these broader forces.

The Geography of Mortality

New South Wales had the highest death toll in Australia in 2022. The majority of these deaths were attributed to internal factors such as heart disease and chronic illnesses.

The map highlights the number of deaths across Australian states in 2022. The states with darker shades represent higher mortality rates, reflecting differences in population and healthcare challenges. These numbers reflect not just individual stories but also underscore the importance of continued efforts to enhance public health and support those in need.

The External Forces behind Life and Death

From the bustling highways to the quiet suburban streets, the roads have claimed the lives of thousands in transport accidents year after year. But the most common, and perhaps the least expected, source of loss is falls—particularly affecting the elderly, highlighting the vulnerabilities that come with aging. As the years progress, the increase in fall-related deaths becomes a stark reminder of the growing needs of an aging population and the importance of preventive care. Drowning, while smaller in numbers, continues to be an ever-present danger, especially in a country where water is central to lifestyle and leisure. Each summer brings new risks, particularly to those unprepared for the power of nature’s waters.

The Hidden Toll of Illness

On the other hand, every year, Australia also faces the profound impact of chronic diseases on its population. Diseases of the circulatory system, such as heart attacks, strokes, and more, claim the highest toll. Yet the year slider reflects that cancer has always closely followed, growing more deadly than all other diseases as of 2013. Mental and behavioral disorders have also grown as a significant cause of death, reflecting the ongoing challenges in mental health awareness and treatment.

A Closer Look at Digestive Cancers

Over the past decade in Australia, a troubling trend has emerged, as visualised through the donut chart, regarding digestive cancers. The data shows a consistent rise in the incidence of these cancers, with pancreatic cancer maintaining a prominent position among the deadliest. While other cancers, like colon cancer, have fluctuated in prevalence, the increasing rates of pancreatic and liver cancers highlight a significant shift in the landscape of digestive cancers. Notably, New South Wales has experienced particularly high rates, indicating a localised concentration of cases. The dataset, filtered by year and state, provides a clearer view of the deadliest cancers within this category.

Pancreatic cancer is the deadliest digestive cancer in Australia, with rising mortality rates due to late detection and lack of research.

Unveiling Diabetes as a Silent Killer

The dataset reveals alarming trends in diabetes-related deaths across Australia from 2012 to 2022. Non-insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus consistently accounts for the highest fatalities, peaking at 2,891 deaths in 2022, highlighting its growing prevalence, especially among older Australians. In contrast, insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus shows lower and more stable death counts, indicating effective management. 
 
By using the States Selection filter, it is evident that New South Wales mirrors national trends, with non-insulin-dependent diabetes leading in mortality rates, increasing from 462 deaths in 2012 to 864 in 2022. Other states also exhibit similar patterns, depicting the fatality of diabetes.

Morbidity Across Generations in Aging Australia

The stacked bar chart reveals significant age-related mortality trends across Australia, highlighting total deaths across various age groups. In New South Wales, the elderly face the highest toll, with 21,089 deaths among those aged 85-94 in 2022, followed by 16,943 deaths in the 75-84 age group. Victoria shows similar patterns, with 16,246 deaths in the 85-94 age group and 12,631 in the 75-84 range. Even younger populations are impacted, as seen in NSW with 327 deaths among 15-24-year-olds. These findings provide a critical perspective on the demographic shifts in mortality, prompting further exploration into the underlying factors contributing to these trends.
